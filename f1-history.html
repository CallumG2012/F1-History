50s

The Front-Engine Years

  • The first World Championship race was held at Silverstone on 13 May 1950; Giuseppe Farina won it for Alfa Romeo.
  • Juan Manuel Fangio won five titles in the decade — 1951 and then four in a row, 1954 to 1957 — across three different constructors, a versatility no champion has matched since.
  • Cars were front-engined and drivers raced without seatbelts or fireproof suits. A Grand Prix was as much a test of endurance as of speed.
Signature stat
5 titles in 7 years — Juan Manuel Fangio, three constructors
60s

The Engine Moves Behind the Driver

  • Cooper's rear/mid-engined cars made the front-engined grid obsolete almost overnight, winning back-to-back titles in 1959 and 1960.
  • Jim Clark and Lotus redefined how fast — and how elegantly — a Grand Prix car could be driven, taking titles in 1963 and 1965.
  • Jack Brabham became the only driver ever to win a championship in a car bearing his own name, in a Brabham BT19, in 1966.
  • Sponsorship arrived in 1968, when Lotus swapped British Racing Green for Gold Leaf livery — the grid's first fully commercial paint job.
Turning point
1968 — the first sponsored livery replaces national racing colours
70s

Wings, Ground Effect, and JPS Black

  • Lotus's John Player Special black-and-gold livery, unveiled in 1972, became one of motorsport's most recognisable paint schemes on first appearance.
  • Jackie Stewart won three titles while campaigning hard for basic safety standards — barriers, medical cars, closed-face helmets — after losing friends on unprotected circuits.
  • 1976 produced one of the sport's defining rivalries: James Hunt edged Niki Lauda to the title, weeks after Lauda's near-fatal fire at the Nürburgring.
  • The Lotus 78 and 79 introduced ground-effect aerodynamics, using the underside of the car to generate downforce and reshaping design for a generation.
Rivalry of the decade
Hunt vs. Lauda, 1976 — decided by a single point
80s

Turbos, and Over 1,000 Horsepower

  • Qualifying-spec turbo engines reportedly exceeded 1,000 horsepower by the mid-1980s, before turbocharging was banned outright for 1989.
  • Alain Prost and Ayrton Senna became McLaren teammates in 1988 and won 15 of 16 races between them — still one of the most dominant single-season campaigns in the sport's history.
  • Nelson Piquet and Nigel Mansell carried the Williams-Honda flag through a decade defined as much by engine wars as driver rivalries.
Signature stat
15 of 16 — McLaren's win share in 1988
90s

Safety, Reformed

  • Ayrton Senna's death at Imola in 1994 forced the sport's most significant safety reckoning, leading to slower cars, softer circuits, and the FIA's modern safety framework.
  • Michael Schumacher won back-to-back titles with Benetton in 1994 and 1995 — the opening chapter of a career that would reshape the record books.
  • Williams fielded the decade's dominant car, taking titles with Mansell (1992), Prost (1993), Hill (1996) and Villeneuve (1997).
  • Active suspension, traction control and other driver aids were banned for 1994 as part of the safety response.
Turning point
1994 — Imola reshapes safety regulation for good
00s

Ferrari, Red, Everywhere

  • Michael Schumacher and Ferrari won five consecutive drivers' and constructors' titles from 2000 to 2004 — still the sport's longest championship streak.
  • Fernando Alonso ended that run with back-to-back titles for Renault in 2005 and 2006, becoming the youngest champion at the time.
  • Lewis Hamilton arrived in 2007, narrowly missed the title as a rookie, then won it the following year at 23 — then the youngest champion ever.
  • Brawn GP, assembled from Honda's abandoned team just months earlier, won both championships in 2009 — one of the sport's great underdog stories.
Signature stat
5 straight titles — Schumacher & Ferrari, 2000–2004
10s

The Hybrid Era Begins

  • Sebastian Vettel and Red Bull won four consecutive titles from 2010–2013; Vettel became the youngest champion in history in 2010, at 23 years old.
  • New V6 turbo-hybrid power units arrived in 2014, and Mercedes used them to launch a run of eight consecutive constructors' titles, through 2021.
  • Lewis Hamilton and Nico Rosberg — former karting teammates turned title rivals — fought a bitter internal Mercedes battle that Rosberg won in 2016, then immediately retired.
  • The Halo cockpit-protection device was introduced in 2018 and has since been credited with saving lives in several major accidents.
Signature stat
8 straight constructors' titles — Mercedes, 2014–2021
20s

A New Era, Reset

  • The 2021 title fight between Lewis Hamilton and Max Verstappen came down to a contested final lap in Abu Dhabi — still one of the most debated finishes in the sport's history.
  • Verstappen went on to win four consecutive titles, 2021–2024, before Lando Norris broke McLaren's 17-year drought in 2025, taking the title by two points over Verstappen at the season finale.
  • 2026 opened a new technical era: a fifty-fifty electric-to-combustion power split, no MGU-H, fully sustainable fuel, and active aerodynamics on every car.
  • Cadillac joined as the sport's 11th team in 2026 — the first entirely new constructor since Haas in 2016 — expanding the grid to 22 cars for the first time in over a decade.
Where things stand
2025 champion: Lando Norris, McLaren — decided by 2 points
